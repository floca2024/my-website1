Collective of Agricultural Workers of Martinique

The Collectif des Ouvriers Agricoles is a movement born from the struggle of agricultural workers in the Antilles, particularly in Martinique and Guadeloupe, against the social, health and environmental injustices they have suffered for decades. Our collective was founded by leading figures of workers' resistance, such as Josette Bomaré and Gisèle Gros , two courageous women who survived the bloody repression of the Chalvet massacre of February 14 , 1974. Their unwavering commitment to the rights of agricultural workers inspired our movement.

Our fight is centered on recognizing the suffering caused by massive exposure to chlordecone , a highly toxic pesticide used in Caribbean banana plantations for over 20 years, despite the proven dangers to human health and the environment. Thousands of farm workers have been contaminated and continue to suffer serious illnesses due to this exposure.

Today, the Agricultural Workers Collective is fighting for:

  • Official recognition of all occupational diseases (male, female and child) linked to exposure to chlordecone,
  • Fair compensation for farm workers and their families,
  • Concrete actions for the decontamination and rehabilitation of contaminated land,
  • Access to appropriate medical care for victims.

We are the voices of the forgotten in Caribbean agriculture , those who have been sacrificed in the name of productivity and profit. Thanks to the strength and resilience of its members, our collective continues to fight for justice and to build a healthier and more equitable future for future generations.

Since the late 1960s, our lands, our rivers, our coastal waters, and our bodies have been poisoned by pesticides, among the most dangerous and polluting of which is chlordecone, all in the name of profit. Our lives have been sacrificed by large plantation owners, under the auspices of the French state, for the sake of colonial monocultures, specifically banana, sugar cane, and pineapple, whose production is meant to meet the needs of France and Europe. The first victims of the scandal, not only of chlordecone, but of pesticides, who are agricultural workers and their beneficiaries, are the great forgotten ones of the public health plans relating to chlordecone. Our populations and territories known as "overseas" are only perceived through a utilitarian and colonial prism by successive governments and by the dominant class of which the large planters are a part. This perception is the same one that allowed the chlordecone scandal to occur, its consequences to continue and large planters to continue the unreasonable use of pesticides. During the Interministerial Committee for Overseas Territories, held in July 2023, the former Prime Minister declared that "the Overseas Territories are an unexpected opportunity for France". The reverse is clearly not true. More than 90% of Martinicans and Guadeloupeans are poisoned by Chlordecone, and, in the absence of a major decontamination plan, this sad state of affairs will continue for centuries to come according to scientists' predictions n the space of 14 years, four "Chlordecone Plans" have been announced. They have proven to be sterile and unsuitable in the face of the immensity of the environmental, health, social and economic disaster that our country is experiencing. None of the measures seriously addressed the needs of the victims, even though they were clearly formulated by the network of associations, civil society and local elected officials. A year ago, 17 years after the filing of a complaint for the crime of chlordecone poisoning, the French courts issued a dismissal order, due to an alleged statute of limitations on the facts being prosecuted. We refuse that the French State, judge and party in this case, arrogates to itself the right to absolve itself and to absolve the large planters of the colonial crimes suffered by agricultural workers. It is therefore more necessary than ever to support the work initiated by Martinique parliamentarians so that a Program Law is submitted to the vote of the French national representation so that wait-and-see and sterile public policies give way to:

•⁠ ⁠Compensation for physical, moral and anxiety damage suffered by all populations of Martinique and Guadeloupe;
•⁠ ⁠Recognition and compensation for ALL occupational diseases resulting from contamination by chlordecone and the “cocktail” of pesticides, by the population and particularly by agricultural workers and their children;

•⁠ ⁠Substantial funding to ensure the operation of the Martinique Regional Center for Occupational and Environmental Pathologies.

We do not tolerate the fact that the large planters responsible for the poisoning (who had the audacity to join the civil action) receive public funds as "compensation for the damages suffered" while the main victims are completely left behind. The big banana growers are behind the manufacturing and marketing of chlordecone, which they have imposed on agricultural workers. They must be forced to assume their share of responsibility. We do not tolerate the flagrant difference in treatment between the victims of the health scandals that have affected France (asbestos, contaminated blood, etc.) and the victims of poisoning in Martinique and Guadeloupe. This discrimination is unacceptable! We do not tolerate the poisoning continuing, and the past contamination with chlordecone affecting us for centuries to come. As revealed by COAADEP, workers are still exposed to pesticides contained in banana washing water in hangars (mainly in the North Atlantic and in central Martinique). Water taken directly from rivers and which, as revealed by the Martinique Water Observatory in 2021, contains 44 pesticides, sometimes at very high levels.

More generally, the massive use of pesticides other than chlordecone continues because it is inseparable from the productivist model and monoculture imposed in agriculture.

Reparation for the crime of poisoning requires an alternative development model and, above all, non-negotiable respect for our humanity.

Intersectionality is a framework to understand how persons, or groups of people, are disadvantaged/advantaged by multiple sources of prejudice/power and discrimination, due to their uniquely overlapping structured identities and experiences such as race, class, gender, age, sexuality, disabilities, migration status and geopolitical location. Therefore, we cannot look at oppression/privilege from “just” a gender or a race perspective, but we always have to take into account the intersections. We give thanks, respect and recognition for our increased awareness to black anti-racist, feminist and queer theorists and activists for intersectionality’s origin.

Transformative cross-community, cross-sector and cross-issue collaborations cannot occur without an intersectional analysis. Intersectionality provides analytical and practical tools for everyone because we are all impacted by oppressive structures. We think that activists, advocates, lawyers, artists, scholars, cultural workers, farmers and everyone else involved in the climate justice movement can use intersectionality to understand and work alongside each other.
ASEED commits to embed intersectionality in our work by putting it into practice and applying it as an organising principle. ASEED recognizes the need to embed an intersectional approach in the way we organize and act as a social movement aiming for agricultural justice, climate justice and food autonomy and overall systemic justice.
ASEED acknowledges that the very concepts/practices which we strive for (agricultural justice, climate justice, and food autonomy) are not concepts and visions solely made by white/western climate movements originally (as which it often seems to appear). These practices have been commonplace long before within indigenous and peasant cultivation practices all over the world.

Interlocking systems of power and oppression are likely to be reproduced even by those of us who strive for an intersectional approach to creating alternative food systems. This must change and an intersectional approach to organising provides possibilities to reduce power inequalities, exclusion and marginalisation within progressive movements.

System Change, Not Climate Change (Austria)

“System Change, not Climate Change!” …is a slogan used by the global climate justice movement. So if you feel connected to this slogan and stand up for a socio-environmental transformation of our economy and our society, you are basically part of the movement. … is also the name for our Austrian climate justice movment. March 28 – Just days before the first round of the presidential election – hundreds of organizations are coming together to build a different future. To make the next five years ones of justice, climate, equality and peace, many social movements are gathering in the streets on April 9, all over France.

The last five years have witnessed rising inequality, a global health crisis, and an amplification of climate change. While this period could have been an opportunity to act to move towards a more just society, it has seen racist discourse proliferate, and women, children, and gender minorities suffer ever more discrimination and sexual violence [1]. However, recent years have also witnessed the rise of social movements, all of which advocate for a future that meets climate challenges and for equality. 

For the very first time, feminist, anti-racist, anti-precarious, climate and social justice associations and collectives are joining forces. Together, they are calling for "Marches for the Future " on Saturday, April 9, in Paris and throughout France, on the eve of the first round of the presidential election. The name of the march is a clear signal: the future, whether it is the next elections or the evolution of society, is up to citizens to write it.

For five years and like never before, social movements have mobilized hundreds of thousands of people: 150,000 during the NousToutes march against sexist and sexual violence in 2019, 350,000 demonstrators at the “march of the century” for the climate in March 2019, 120,000 people at the demonstration of June 13, 2020 organized by the Adama Committee against police violence. “ We cannot leave the country in this state ,” assures Assa Traoré, of the Adama Committee. It is important to march, with our differences and all united, to fight against all discrimination and forms of violence and demand equality and justice .” “ We fight hand in hand because all these themes are intertwined ,” explains Elodie Nace, spokesperson for Alternatiba. Combating the climate emergency and systemic violence can only be done by equally strongly defending equality and social justice .” 

However, these subjects occupy a very small place in the media [2] and are the great forgotten of the presidential campaign. But nothing is decided. On the eve of the first round, the organizers of these marches want to remind the electorate that these subjects concern all of society and to the candidates that they will be present for the next five years. "With the increase in precariousness linked to the crisis, the poor energy performance of 5 to 7 million colander-like homes and the increase in energy and rent costs, there is an emergency, warns Manuel Domergue, Director of Studies at the Abbé Pierre Foundation. The share of households feeling cold at home has increased from 14% in 2020 to 20% in 2021. The poorest are now paying for the shortcomings of housing renovation policies." 

If social movements are uniting today, it is because the violence they are fighting has common roots and similar modes of operation. " We cannot waste another five years. There is an urgent need to act for the climate, against racist violence and gender-based violence suffered by women, children and LGBTQIA+, believes Pauline Baron, from the #NousToutes Collective. All these forms of violence reinforce each other, so we will only be able to completely and definitively end one by destroying the others ." The end of this system of oppression can only be achieved by fighting together.

The organizations are therefore calling for people to come together on Saturday, April 9 throughout France and in Paris (2 p.m. at Bastille) to make the next five years those of justice, climate, equality and peace. We cannot wait five more years to build our future.

We are saying ‘goodbye’ to the name Black Earth 🖤

▪️Throughout this last year, we have grown a lot as a collective - not in numbers, but definitely in strength and connection. We know each other better, we care for each other more deeply, and we are getting better at having uncomfortable conversations that help us grow both individually and collectively. We have established a stronger focus on collective care, collaboration, and solidarity 🫂 We’ve initiated new projects and nurtured various connections with other groups grounded in Berlin-based movements for liberation globally. We desire to keep exploring what our work as a collective based in Germany can and should look like. 💫

▪️This growth and process results from our collective navigating a disagreement related to the name, mission, and representation of Black Earth. Alongside personal tensions, there has been longstanding critique about the name and logo giving the impression of an exclusively Black space while the collective has, from its beginnings, always been a Black-led space open to non-Black people of racialized/marginalized identities in Germany. Those currently active in the collective made the decision to remain open to non-Black racialized comrades who wish to organize for climate justice through a decolonial lens.

▪️In between grief and steadfastness, we honor everyone who helped build Black Earth. We will carry the legacy of each one of us to our future paths.

▪️While we seek a new name and logo that suits the work we wish to do, our political analysis and organizing efforts will remain rooted in an understanding and/or critique of global anti-Blackness, colorism, classism, imperialism, racial capitalism, ableism, cis-hetero-patriarchy and other forms of oppression. We look forward to collaborating with comrades who share this political grounding and to continuing to build strong alliances for collective liberation! ✊🏾

▪️A new chapter also requires new faces and perspectives, and thus, we happily invite new comrades to our collective. If you are interested in joining us, stay tuned! We’ll post more information about how to join our collective within the next days 🫶🏾

Did you know that Eyfa has published many materials?

'Climate justice & accessibility' is a try for decolonial perspectives and guidance on the topics of climate justice & accessibility. The submissions come from individual creators, each person has drawn from their own experiences, beliefs, and practices. This can be seen in the different approaches to decoloniality, dis*ability & climate justice and queerness.

The perspectives included in this zine, can not speak for everyone. Many more views on climate justice and accessibility are still missing, especially from the front lines of these struggles. The main contributors of this zine are mostly people who are based in Europe/UK, sharing their critique on climate movements in these contexts.

When we speak about climate justice movements in global north contexts, this come with the awareness that there are
much bigger environmental and climate justice movements on the African continent e.g. Ogoni protests in the 1990s with more than 300.000 people. And it comes with the acknowledgement for all the anti-colonial struggles since centuries for land and people all around the globe.

Regarding of accessibility of this zine itself: graphic & written (online) content is not accessible to everyone. The zine is written in the colonial language English, some contributors speak non- colonial languages too. Texts have sometimes colloquial language, academic language and sometimes simple language. Some terms (and content) within the zine will become outdated/obsolete and evolve with time. This is about learning and unlearning.

A very special thanks to Boaz Murinzi Murena, Shahd K, Dee Woods, Samuel Fabian, muSa Michelle Mattiuzzi, and everyone who contributed to this zine in any way. You made this zine possible!

Youth Organisations Decolonizing Climate Justice & Activism

!!!! This activity is specific for persons based in Germany and Spain!!!! - write us for more infos.

International Youth Exchange
14th - 20th august 2023
Groß Kreutz , Brandenburg – Germany

For the duration of one week in mid August, we will dive into the meaning of climate justice in the European context.
Through various workshops, games and other activities we'll explore decolonial perspectives on environmental & climate (in)justice, anti-oppression work, food justice and non-western concepts of conservation

Through our week-long journey we will establish a common ground and hopefully dive deeper into these concepts and ideas. We want to dream together and finally build, and plan a follow-up project to strengthen our movements. It is important for us to provide space and time for discussion, reading, socializing, quiet time, observations and reflection.

Our goal is to provide a space where young people within the climate justice movement in Europe can learn with and from one another and foster exchange in order to build resilient and proactive structures. With this we aim to contribute to a movement that is led by the most affected communities and acts motivated by reciprocity and care.

who is this for ?
We strongly want to encourage BIPoC* (Black, Indigenous, People of Colour) that have an intersectional experience with various forms of discrimination (e.g. ableism, queer-‚transphobia, classism,…) to apply.

The week is mainly designed for people who are passionate about climate justice and (want to) engage in movement spaces. If you are new to these fields, you are still warmly welcome to apply. There is no pre-required knowledge/ experience standards.

Participants must be based in Germany and Spain.

*This term includes every person, that experiences racism and/or anti-semitismus.

costs and support
The whole event will be free of charge for all participants. Accommodation, food & travel will be covered.

This activity is a collaboration with @blackearthkollektiv and in partnership with Project Ulex.
